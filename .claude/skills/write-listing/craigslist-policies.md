# Craigslist flagging — field guide

Compiled 2026-07 from ~a dozen threads in Craigslist's flag help forum (forums.craigslist.org, forumID=3), including "why flag our housing post?", "Want feedback on new version of room share ad", "Shared room ad keeps getting removed", "Made adjustments and still system takes it down", and "Roommate ad Flagged two times".
This is peer advice from experienced forum members (including ex-flaggers), not official Craigslist policy — CL staff "isn't telling us what triggers their system," so treat it as educated inference.
Removal is "usually due to multiple issues, not just one" — fixing a single thing and reposting rarely works.

## Diagnostic frame: how fast did it disappear?

Removed within minutes → an automated filter caught it.
Removed after hours or days → human community flagging played a role.
The two require different fixes.

## 1. Technical and account-level triggers (automated, wording-independent)

- **Reusing an old ad.** Recycling the same ad every vacancy trips filters as an "unfixed retry ad." Write a genuinely fresh ad each time — not just rearranged sentences ("you're now trying to please an algorithm... gotta fool them").
- **Duplicate content across sites.** The same text on any other platform (SpareRoom, Facebook, own website) trips filters. "Write a totally unique ad for this site."
- **Ad fatigue.** Even an ad that ran fine for years can start getting pulled if it's substantially the same.
- **Connection/IP flags.** VPNs, spammy public WiFi, excessive privacy settings.
- **Not being local.** Posting to an area you aren't physically in can trip filters. (Relevant: operator posts LA listings from NYC.)
- **Cascade effect.** Once an ad is removed, subsequent ads are removed more easily — get it right rather than rapid-fire reposting.

## 2. Fair-housing / discrimination triggers (biggest content theme)

Filters "don't read nuances or explanations — they pick up on certain words and phrases."

- **Keep the ad about the housing, not the people.** "Housing is ALL about the housing" — race, sexual orientation, age, and similar attributes don't belong.
- **No preference language.** "Woman preferred" / "woman only" gets flagged; instead state a neutral fact ("2 women live here") and screen at the interview.
- **No "pop quiz."** No screening questions or eligibility requirements in the ad text; save all questions for the interview.
- **Careful with income requirements.** "Paycheck stubs" reads as discriminatory (excludes retired/disabled/alimony-supported applicants); ask for proof of income via bank statements instead.
- You can still choose whom to rent to — you just can't discriminate in the ad itself.

## 3. Content quality and formatting

- **Cut the length.** Long ads trip filters; one member advised losing ~80% of a bloated ad.
- **No bullet points** — they can trigger filters.
- **Lose realtor speak.** Drop boast words (charming, lovely, cozy, vibrant, elegant); stick to concrete specifics — square footage, flooring, parking, appliances, laundry.
- **Clear, specific title.** No jargon ("2br Double"), no "available now" in the title.
- **Don't hide info.** Leaving key fields blank makes an ad look "at best marginal."

## 4. Legitimacy and legal-detail signals

- **State your relationship to the property.** Not required, but writing "owner/landlord" avoids looking like a lease-breaking tenant or hired scammer.
- **Only advertise a legal bedroom.** Four sealed walls, proper egress (door plus window or separate exit), heat/air.
- **No illegal fees.** Non-refundable move-in fees may be illegal by state; security deposits are often capped (~one month's rent).

## Content recipe for a compliant room-for-rent ad

- ~60% the room: square footage, windows, closets, furnished or not, private vs. shared bath.
- ~20% the home: square footage, parking, laundry, appliances, A/C, smoking policy.
- ~20% the roommates: neutral basics only (number of people), plus move-in cost and general area/cross-streets.

Everything else — screening, personal questions, preferences — happens at the interview.

The fair-housing points are genuine legal compliance, not just filter-gaming: the safest ad is one that's actually non-discriminatory, not one that merely dodges keywords.
